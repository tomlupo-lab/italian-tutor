# Fast Italian Roadmap Reference

## Purpose

This document defines the preferred curriculum spine for Italian Tutor.

Related documents:

- [core-italian-sentence-patterns.md](/home/twilc/projects/italian-tutor/italian-tutor/docs/core-italian-sentence-patterns.md)
- [curriculum-content-model.md](/home/twilc/projects/italian-tutor/italian-tutor/docs/curriculum-content-model.md)
- [content-review-checklist.md](/home/twilc/projects/italian-tutor/italian-tutor/docs/content-review-checklist.md)

It favors a fast, functional path to usable Italian over exhaustive grammar coverage.
The goal is not to teach everything in CEFR order. The goal is to get the learner to practical A1, A2, and B1 performance as quickly as possible using high-yield grammar, high-frequency vocabulary, and reusable sentence patterns.

This roadmap should be treated as:

- the default learning progression reference
- the baseline for backend inventory audits
- the standard for SRS card quality
- the recommended language-preparation model for mission design

The sentence-pattern reference defines the reusable language units that operationalize this roadmap.
The content model defines how those decisions should be represented in content records and exercise design.
The review checklist defines how content should be evaluated against this roadmap.

This roadmap is primarily a curriculum and content-design reference. It does not require the app to hard-gate mission access by phase or pattern readiness.

## Core Philosophy

Italian Tutor should optimize for the 80/20 path:

- teach the smallest grammar set that unlocks real communication
- prioritize high-frequency words and chunks
- prefer sentence patterns over isolated vocabulary
- push listening early
- delay advanced or low-yield grammar until later

The app should aim to build functional independence first, then deepen precision.

## Design Principles

### 1. Chunk-first over word-list-first

Prefer:

- `Ho bisogno di...`
- `Mi fa male...`
- `Vorrei prenotare...`
- `Può ripetere?`

Over isolated items that are hard to deploy in conversation.

### 2. Grammar must earn its place

A structure should be introduced early only if it materially improves:

- survival communication
- daily task completion
- narration of real events
- discussion of plans and preferences

### 3. Missions must depend on taught language

Mission difficulty should be informed by the fast roadmap. Missions can still be filtered primarily by CEFR level in the product, but their content should not drift far beyond the language behavior the roadmap is trying to establish.

### 4. Inventory quality is coverage plus usefulness

A strong inventory is not just large. It must:

- cover the right high-frequency domains
- reinforce the current grammar core
- avoid low-value early content
- produce review cards that teach reusable language

## Fast Progression Model

## Phase 1: Survival Italian

Estimated effort:

- roughly 40 to 50 hours

Primary goal:

- simple interactions and self-expression

### Grammar

- present tense of regular verbs
- `essere`
- `avere`
- modal verbs: `potere`, `dovere`, `volere`
- articles
- basic prepositions

### Core verb priority

The early system should heavily reinforce a compact high-frequency verb set such as:

- `essere`
- `avere`
- `fare`
- `dire`
- `andare`
- `vedere`
- `prendere`
- `dare`
- `stare`
- `venire`
- `trovare`
- `pensare`
- `parlare`
- `lavorare`
- `mangiare`
- `bere`
- `comprare`
- `arrivare`
- `partire`
- `restare`
- `vivere`

### Vocabulary domains

Focus on the smallest useful set:

- greetings
- identity
- countries and nationalities
- work
- food and drink
- numbers
- time
- places in the city
- transport basics
- home basics

### Communicative outcomes

The learner should be able to:

- introduce themselves
- ask for basic things
- order food and drinks
- ask simple location and price questions
- describe basic daily facts

### Example output

```text
Mi chiamo Tom.
Vivo a Varsavia.
Lavoro in finanza.
Posso prendere un caffè?
Quanto costa?
```

## Phase 2: Real Daily Conversations

Estimated effort:

- roughly 60 to 120 hours total

Primary goal:

- handle normal daily-life situations

### Grammar

Add only the next high-yield structures:

- `passato prossimo`
- reflexive verbs
- direct object pronouns
- comparatives

### Vocabulary domains

Expand into practical daily life:

- travel
- restaurants
- shopping
- housing
- transport
- family
- work
- emotions
- routine
- pharmacy and doctor

Target vocabulary at this stage should remain tightly focused on high-frequency use.

### Communicative outcomes

The learner should be able to:

- describe what happened
- manage common service interactions
- ask for clarification
- solve basic travel and shopping problems
- discuss routine and immediate needs

### Example output

```text
Ieri ho lavorato molto.
Ho visto un film.
Può portarmi il conto?
Me lo può ripetere?
```

## Phase 3: Functional Independence

Estimated effort:

- roughly 200 to 300 hours total

Primary goal:

- explain experiences, plans, and reasons with connected speech

### Grammar

Add the next essential layer:

- `imperfetto`
- `futuro semplice`
- conditional, especially `vorrei`
- relative clauses, especially `che`

### Vocabulary domains

Extend into broader independent use:

- life events
- travel stories
- plans and goals
- education
- work projects
- technology
- opinions
- culture and society

### Communicative outcomes

The learner should be able to:

- narrate past events with basic contrast
- discuss future plans
- express wants and hypothetical preferences
- explain opinions in simple connected language

### Example output

```text
Quando ero studente lavoravo molto.
L'anno prossimo viaggerò in Italia.
Vorrei vivere lì.
Penso che sia importante imparare le lingue.
```

## Minimal Grammar Set For Fast B1

This is the core grammar set that should drive early app content:

| Grammar | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Present tense | covers most basic conversation |
| `Passato prossimo` | lets learners report events |
| `Imperfetto` | adds background and description |
| Future | supports plans and intent |
| Modal verbs | essential for requests and needs |
| Pronouns | necessary for natural everyday speech |
| Comparatives | useful for describing and choosing |
| Relative clauses | enables longer connected sentences |

Everything else should be delayed unless a strong product reason exists.

Examples of grammar to delay:

- advanced subjunctive coverage
- rare verb forms
- literary or low-frequency structures

## Vocabulary Policy

The app should prefer:

- high-frequency words
- high-frequency chunks
- domain vocabulary that appears in missions and real daily tasks

The app should avoid:

- low-frequency themed vocabulary introduced too early
- overly abstract vocabulary before B1
- SRS cards whose back side is metadata or error traces instead of teachable language

### Target vocabulary ranges

These are directional, not strict certification claims:

| Stage | Approx useful vocabulary |
| --- | --- |
| Early A1 | 400 to 600 |
| Strong A1 | 600 to 800 |
| A2 | 1200 to 1500 |
| Functional B1 | 2500 to 3000 |

## SRS Guidance

The preferred review unit is the reusable chunk or short sentence, not the isolated noun.

Preferred card patterns:

1. chunk -> meaning
2. sentence -> meaning
3. audio -> transcription or meaning
4. fill-in grammar

Examples:

Front:

```text
Ho bisogno di...
```

Back:

```text
I need...
```

Front:

```text
Ieri ____ mangiato una pizza.
```

Back:

```text
ho
```

Front:

```text
Mi fa male...
```

Back:

```text
It hurts...
```

## Listening Priority

Listening should start early and remain a core part of the loop.

Reason:

- it accelerates pattern recognition
- it reinforces grammar without explicit study overload
- it improves recall of chunks and common turns of phrase
- it supports real mission performance better than text-only study

## Suggested Daily Loop

For a fast-functional track:

- 20 minutes SRS
- 20 minutes listening
- 10 minutes grammar
- 10 minutes speaking or writing

This is the default study loop the product should optimize around.

## Product Implications

This roadmap implies the following for app design.

### Mission design

- missions should be gated by language already covered in the fast roadmap
- early missions should emphasize survival tasks and predictable sentence patterns
- A1 missions should not quietly require A2/B1 reasoning or negotiation depth

### Exercise generation

- generated content should reinforce current high-yield grammar
- generated content should prioritize high-frequency domains
- content should be rejected or deprioritized if it introduces low-value complexity too early

### Recovery drills

- recovery should focus on common breakdowns in core structures
- drills should repair high-frequency failures first
- recovery content should produce reusable language, not exercise artifacts

### Inventory audits

Inventory quality should be evaluated against:

- grammar coverage by phase
- vocabulary coverage by domain
- chunk usefulness
- mission readiness
- learner-level appropriateness

## Timeline Expectations

Directional expectations for a committed learner:

| Target | Typical pace |
| --- | --- |
| A1 | about 6 weeks |
| A2 | about 4 months |
| B1 | about 8 to 10 months |

With high daily immersion, B1 may be achievable faster, but the app should not make promises based on best-case timelines.

## Summary

Italian Tutor should treat this fast roadmap as the primary curriculum reference.

In short:

- teach less, but teach the right things
- prioritize chunks over isolated words
- prioritize high-frequency grammar over complete grammar
- prioritize listening early
- make missions prove useful language, not replace structured progression

This is the preferred standard for future curriculum, inventory, and quality decisions.
